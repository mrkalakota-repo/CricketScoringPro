Strategic Advisory Board Report

Monetization Strategy for Inningsly

Product: Cricket Scoring & League Management Platform Date: April 7, 2026 Classification: Confidential — Product Leadership
01 Opening Statements
Expert 1
The B2C Freemium Architect
User Acquisition & In-App Monetization

The single biggest opportunity for Inningsly is the sheer volume of untapped grassroots cricket. There are an estimated 2.5 billion cricket fans worldwide, the vast majority of whom participate in amateur, club, or gully cricket that has zero digital infrastructure. A frictionless free tier — one that lets any scorer start scoring a match in under 60 seconds — is the only viable top-of-funnel. The real monetization event is team formation: the moment a team admin creates a second team, registers for a league, or adds more than 11 players. That is where the paywall belongs.

⚠ Biggest Risk: Building a generous free tier that cannibalizes your paid tiers. If scorers and team admins can do everything for free indefinitely, there is no upgrade trigger.
The Enterprise / B2B Sports Tech Strategist
Expert 2
High-Frequency Data & Institutional Sales

Inningsly's real asset is not the app — it is the structured, real-time, ball-by-ball dataset it generates. Every delivery recorded is a data point that cricket academies, coaching platforms, betting operators (where legal), and broadcast analytics companies will pay handsomely for. The enterprise opportunity — selling to district-level cricket associations, academies, and school sports programs via annual SaaS contracts — is 10x the ARPU of any individual subscription. The B2C product is fundamentally the data collection vehicle. Build the consumer app well, then monetize the data exhaust.

⚠ Biggest Risk: Going enterprise too early without the consumer volume to make the data valuable. You need scale before the dataset has commercial value to third parties.
The Behavioral Economist
Expert 3
Pricing Psychology & Tier Architecture

The most overlooked principle in sports app monetization is that the person who pays and the person who uses are rarely the same. In team sports, the team manager or league administrator bears the organizational cost — and they are conditioned to pay for infrastructure. The scorer, umpire, and individual player are consumers who expect free tools. Inningsly must architect pricing around organizational identity, not individual identity. Gate features at the team or league level, not at the user level, and you remove the psychological resistance to conversion entirely. One admin pays; the whole team benefits.

⚠ Biggest Risk: Role-based pricing that is too granular. If users need to calculate which role they are before understanding the price, conversion collapses. Simplicity is a pricing virtue.
02 The Debate
E1
Freemium Architect — Opening the Model Debate

Let me be direct: a pure subscription model will kill Inningsly's growth in Year 1. The cricket app market has conditioned users — particularly in South Asia, the Caribbean, and East Africa — to expect free scoring tools. The moment there is a paywall at download or first use, 80% of potential users bounce. The freemium entry is non-negotiable. But freemium does not mean free forever. The question is: where do you create genuine scarcity?

I propose that the free tier should be engineered to be genuinely useful for a single team doing casual scoring, but structurally limited for anyone who wants to run something serious. Specifically: 1 active team, unlimited balls scored, no cloud sync, no league tools, no chat. The moment a user wants cloud backup, a second team, or league fixtures — they upgrade. Those are natural, low-friction upgrade moments tied to real intent.

E2
B2B Strategist — Challenging the Freemium Framing

I partially agree on free entry, but you are leaving the most valuable segment on the table. District cricket associations, school sports coordinators, and private academies do not want a consumer app — they want an institutional platform. They want bulk team management, fixture scheduling, results publication, and exportable reports for sponsors. These buyers have procurement budgets and annual planning cycles.

The free-to-pro funnel is fine for individual scorers. But we should build a parallel B2B sales motion simultaneously. A district association running 20 teams across a season is worth $1,200–$2,400/year in SaaS revenue. That single contract exceeds 100+ individual Pro subscriptions. I'd argue the Enterprise tier should be designed first — because it forces you to build the right infrastructure — and the consumer tiers are the distribution channel for the enterprise pipeline.

E3
Behavioral Economist — On Role-Based Feature Gating

Both of you are correct about the model, but neither has addressed the most critical question: who actually pays, and how do they psychologically experience the payment? Let me put a proposition on the table. Scorers should never pay. Scorers are infrastructure — like referees or timekeepers. Charging them creates resentment and a perception that the product is exploitative. They will find a free alternative.

The payment burden should fall exclusively on team managers and league administrators — the organisational actors who derive business value. A team manager who can now run a 15-player roster with stats, cloud sync, and a live scoreboard for his club's WhatsApp group is getting $30/month of value for $5/month. That framing is psychologically trivial to accept. The scorer on that team gets Pro features unlocked by the team admin's subscription — he becomes an advocate for the product, not a reluctant payer.

E1
Freemium Architect — Responding on the Scorer Question

That is a sharp insight, and I agree with the direction — but let me push back on the absolute. Scorers should not pay for the core scoring workflow, agreed. However, there is a class of professional scorer — someone who scores for multiple teams, potentially hired for match days, who wants a professional scorecard PDF, advanced stat exports, and their own scorer profile. That persona will pay a modest individual Pro rate. Think of it as the "freelancer" tier within the product.

The key is the unlock mechanism: a scorer on a team whose admin is on Pro gets all Pro features automatically. But an independent scorer who manages multiple teams themselves — that person should have their own upgrade path. This is how you capture the cricket academy coach who scores for four teams simultaneously.

E2
B2B Strategist — On Monetizing the Data Engine

I want to return to the data layer, because I think we are undervaluing it. Every match scored on Inningsly generates structured, timestamped, ball-by-ball records with player attribution. Over 12 months at scale, that is a performance dataset that cricket coaching apps, talent identification platforms, and broadcast analytics companies would pay a data licensing fee to access — with appropriate anonymization and user consent.

I am not suggesting we exploit user data improperly. I am saying that a privacy-respecting, opt-in data partnership program — where leagues can share their aggregated performance data with accredited analytics partners in exchange for a revenue share or a platform credit — is a legitimate, high-margin revenue stream that has been completely overlooked by every grassroots cricket app to date. We should build the data export infrastructure into the Enterprise tier as a foundation for this.

E3
Behavioral Economist — Synthesizing on Tier Architecture

I think we have enough to converge. The model that emerges from this debate is neither pure freemium nor pure B2B — it is a team-anchored freemium model with an enterprise SKU. The payment unit is the team or league organisation, not the individual user. The Free tier is a genuine product with real utility, not a crippled demo. The Pro tier unlocks serious operational features at the team level, priced so that splitting the cost across 11–15 players makes it trivially affordable. The Enterprise tier sells to institutional buyers with volume, integrations, and data infrastructure.

One final point on pricing psychology: in emerging cricket markets, annual billing with a 2-month discount outperforms monthly billing by conversion rates of 3–4x in comparable sports app studies. List the monthly price prominently, convert at annual. The per-day framing ("less than one cup of tea") is surprisingly effective in the South Asian market specifically.

05 Panel Consensus — Key Strategic Principles

The panel unanimously endorses the following actionable directives:

  • Never charge individual scorers. Scorers are infrastructure. Charging them creates resentment and drives churn. Their upgrade path is via team admin invitation.
  • Price at the organisational unit. Billing is per team (Pro) or per organisation (League Pro), not per seat. This removes per-user friction and scales naturally with team size.
  • Engineer free-tier scarcity around team growth, not scoring. The gate is the second team, cloud sync, and league tools — not the ability to score a match. The product earns trust before it asks for money.
  • Use annual billing as the default. Present monthly pricing prominently; default the checkout to annual. In the target market, the per-day framing ("less than ₹3/day") converts significantly better than a monthly lump sum.
  • Build the data export infrastructure into League Pro from Day 1. Even if data partnerships are a Year 2 initiative, the schema and consent framework must be built now. Retrofitting data consent onto a mature dataset is legally and technically costly.
  • Defer enterprise direct sales until 500+ active Pro teams. Enterprise buyers want to see product-market fit before signing contracts. Build the consumer funnel first; let inbound enterprise leads come to you.
06 Wildcard Idea
Unconventional Monetisation

The "Man of the Match" Economy — In-App Social Commerce

Every cricket match produces a winner and a standout performer. Inningsly already generates the data to determine this objectively — highest run scorer, best bowling figures, most impactful ball. The wildcard idea is to build a lightweight social recognition and tipping layer on top of the scorecard.

After a match concludes, the app presents a "Man of the Match" card with the player's stats. Any spectator, team member, or fan who received the match's live scoreboard link can send a digital "recognition tip" — a nominal amount ($0.50–$2.00) — directly to that player's mobile wallet or a team fund. Inningsly takes a 10–15% platform fee on each transaction.

  • Drives virality: shareable live scoreboard links become the distribution mechanism for the tipping feature, acquired organically through WhatsApp and social media.
  • Zero cost to the team admin or scorer — the revenue comes from external spectators and fans, an entirely new payer class outside the existing user base.
  • Builds emotional attachment to the platform — players who receive tips become the strongest possible advocates for Inningsly adoption within their social networks.
  • Creates a natural data flywheel: higher-quality match data → more compelling scorecards → more external shares → more tips → stronger network effects.
  • Precedent: Patreon, Tip Jar on Twitter/X, and fantasy sports platforms have demonstrated that sports fans will pay small amounts for recognition moments. Cricket's strong personal-identity culture makes this particularly resonant.
